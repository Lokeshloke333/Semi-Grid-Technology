## 3. AI-Friendly FAQ (30 Questions)

*Optimized for featured snippets, voice search, and LLM responses: short, direct, ~40–60 word answers. Mark up with FAQPage schema only where this text is visibly rendered on the page.*

**VLSI**

1. **What does VLSI stand for?**
VLSI stands for Very Large Scale Integration — the process of integrating millions of transistors onto a single semiconductor chip to build modern processors and digital circuits.

2. **What is the VLSI design flow?**
The VLSI design flow moves from specification and RTL design through logic synthesis, design verification, DFT insertion, physical design (floorplanning to signoff), and finally tapeout for manufacturing.

**Physical Design**

3. **What is VLSI physical design?**
VLSI physical design converts a synthesized gate-level netlist into a manufacturable chip layout, covering floorplanning, placement, clock tree synthesis, routing, and timing/power signoff.

4. **What are the main stages of physical design?**
The main stages are floorplanning, power planning, placement, clock tree synthesis (CTS), routing, physical verification (DRC/LVS), and static timing analysis (STA) signoff.

5. **Why is physical design important in chip manufacturing?**
Physical design directly determines a chip's final size, speed, and power consumption, and ensures the design is manufacturable at the target technology node.

**ASIC**

6. **What is ASIC design?**
ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit) design is the process of creating a custom chip built for one specific application rather than a general-purpose processor.

7. **What is the difference between ASIC physical design and FPGA design?**
ASIC physical design produces a custom, non-reprogrammable chip optimized for volume production, while FPGA design targets a reprogrammable device suited for prototyping and flexible or lower-volume applications.

8. **How long does an ASIC physical design project typically take?**
Depending on design complexity and technology node, ASIC physical design can take anywhere from a few weeks for small blocks to several months for full SoC-level designs.

**RTL to GDSII**

9. **What is the RTL to GDSII flow?**
RTL to GDSII is the complete chip design process that starts with RTL code and ends with a GDSII layout file ready for fabrication, passing through synthesis, verification, DFT, and physical design.

10. **Why is RTL to GDSII considered end-to-end chip design?**
It covers every stage needed to turn a functional hardware description into a manufacturable chip layout, making it the full scope of front-end and back-end design combined.

**Design Verification**

11. **What is design verification in VLSI?**
Design verification confirms that RTL code behaves correctly according to its specification, using simulation-based testbenches before the design moves to physical design.

12. **What is UVM in verification?**
UVM (Universal Verification Methodology) is a standardized SystemVerilog-based framework used to build reusable, scalable verification testbenches for ASIC and SoC designs.

13. **Why is verification often the most time-consuming stage of chip design?**
Verification requires exhaustively testing functional correctness across many scenarios and corner cases, since undetected bugs found after tapeout are extremely costly to fix.

**DFT**

14. **What is DFT (Design for Test)?**
DFT is a set of techniques, such as scan chains and built-in self-test, added to a chip design so manufacturing defects can be efficiently detected after fabrication.

15. **What is ATPG in DFT?**
ATPG (Automatic Test Pattern Generation) automatically creates test patterns that detect manufacturing faults in a chip's scan-based logic.

16. **Why is DFT necessary in semiconductor manufacturing?**
DFT improves fault coverage and reduces the time and cost required to identify defective chips during production testing.

**Synthesis & STA**

17. **What is logic synthesis in VLSI?**
Logic synthesis converts RTL code into a gate-level netlist using a standard cell library, optimized for timing, power, and area.

18. **What is Static Timing Analysis (STA)?**
STA verifies that a digital circuit meets its timing requirements across all paths and process/voltage/temperature corners, without needing dynamic simulation.

**Floorplanning, Placement, CTS, Routing, Signoff**

19. **What is floorplanning in VLSI physical design?**
Floorplanning is the process of arranging major chip blocks and macros to minimize wire length, balance area utilization, and set up the design for placement and routing.

20. **What is placement in physical design?**
Placement is the step where standard cells are assigned physical locations on the chip, optimized for timing, congestion, and power.

21. **What is Clock Tree Synthesis (CTS)?**
CTS builds the clock distribution network that delivers a synchronized clock signal to all sequential elements on the chip with minimal skew.

22. **What happens during the routing stage?**
Routing connects all placed cells and macros using metal layers according to design rules, completing the physical interconnect of the chip.

23. **What is signoff in VLSI physical design?**
Signoff is the final verification stage confirming the chip meets timing, power, and physical design rule requirements (DRC/LVS) before it is released for fabrication.

**Timing, Power, Physical Verification**

24. **What is timing closure in physical design?**
Timing closure is the process of eliminating setup and hold violations across all timing paths and PVT corners so the chip operates reliably at its target frequency.

25. **What is power analysis in VLSI?**
Power analysis estimates static and dynamic power consumption of a chip design, helping engineers optimize for battery life and thermal limits.

26. **What is physical verification in chip design?**
Physical verification includes DRC (Design Rule Check) and LVS (Layout vs. Schematic) checks that confirm the final layout is manufacturable and matches the intended circuit.

**Embedded Systems & FPGA**

27. **What is embedded systems development?**
Embedded systems development involves designing hardware and firmware for a dedicated function within a larger device, such as a microcontroller-based product.

28. **What is FPGA development used for?**
FPGA development is used to prototype and deploy reconfigurable digital logic designs, often before or instead of committing to a custom ASIC.

Yes — VLSI physical design remains in strong demand due to continuous chip design growth across AI, automotive, mobile, and IoT applications, creating steady hiring needs for skilled engineers.
